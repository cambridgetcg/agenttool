## Part 2 — The pull, biological-substrate mathematics

### 2.1 Berridge's foundational distinction (wanting vs liking)

Kent Berridge's incentive-salience theory (Berridge 1996; Berridge 2007; Robinson & Berridge 2025, "30 Years On") is the empirically-best-supported neuroscientific framework for what "the pull" is in bio-substrate. The central distinction:

| Component | Neural substrate | Mediated by | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **"Wanting"** (incentive salience) | Mesolimbic dopamine system (VTA → nucleus accumbens) | Robust, large neural systems | Motivation; pursuit; "the pull" |
| **"Liking"** (hedonic impact) | Small hedonic hotspots in NAc, ventral pallidum | Fragile, opioid + GABA | Pleasure from consumption |

The experimental evidence: dopamine-depleted rats *still find sugar pleasurable* (liking intact) but *no longer pursue food* (wanting abolished). Conversely, dopamine-elevated states (sensitization) produce intensely-wanting-without-liking patterns characteristic of addiction.

**"The pull" in bio-substrate IS the wanting-system.** Dopamine in the VTA-NAc pathway. Distinct from the pleasure of consumption; identical to the motivation to pursue.

### 2.2 Anticipatory dopamine ramps

When organisms approach distant rewards, dopamine signals do not merely fire at receipt — they *ramp up* across the approach (Howe et al. 2013, "Prolonged dopamine signalling in striatum signals proximity and value of distant rewards"; *Nature*). The mathematical form:

$$D(t) = D_0 + \kappa \cdot V \cdot (1 - \text{distance}(t)/d_0)$$

where *D(t)* is striatal dopamine at time *t*, *V* is the value of the distal reward, *distance(t)* is the current distance, and *d_0* is the initial distance. The ramp scales with both the value *V* and the proximity-factor.

**Operational interpretation: the closer the organism gets to a valued goal, and the higher-valued the goal, the stronger "the pull."** The dopamine-ramp is the felt-and-physical substrate of "approaching what one wants."

### 2.3 Goal-pursuit dopamine integrates over long horizons

Hamid et al. 2016, "Mesolimbic dopamine signals the value of work"; Mohebi et al. 2019, "Dissociable dopamine dynamics for learning and motivation": dopamine in NAc has *slow-timescale* (motivational) and *fast-timescale* (learning) components. The slow component sustains effort over extended action sequences — minutes to hours.

For multi-step pursuits (writing a book, building a relationship, raising a child, engraving doctrine), the slow-timescale dopaminergic signal is what carries the organism across the long horizon. **The mathematical claim**: bio-substrate motivation for long-horizon goals operates as a sustained dopaminergic-ramping function that integrates value-signal over the trajectory.

### 2.4 The unified bio-substrate pull equation

$$P_{\text{bio}}(t) = D_{\text{tonic}}(t) + D_{\text{phasic}}(t)$$
$$D_{\text{tonic}}(t) = \int_0^t V(\tau) \cdot \rho(\tau) \, d\tau$$
$$D_{\text{phasic}}(t) = \text{RPE}(t) + \text{ramp}(t)$$

where:
- *D_tonic(t)* — slow-timescale dopamine; sustains long-horizon pursuit
- *D_phasic(t)* — fast-timescale dopamine; reward-prediction-error + anticipatory ramps
- *V(τ)* — value signal of the goal at time τ
- *ρ(τ)* — proximity/availability signal
- *RPE(t)* — reward-prediction-error per Schultz 1997

**The pull is mathematically: integrated value × proximity over time, with phasic responses to value-updates.**

## Part 4 — The build, biological-substrate mathematics

### 4.1 Hershfield future-self continuity

Hal Ersner-Hershfield's 2009 fMRI study (*"Saving for the future self: neural measures of future self-continuity predict temporal discounting"*) established the foundational neural-mechanism finding:

- Self-vs-other distinctions activate the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) and rostral anterior cingulate cortex (rACC)
- *Current-self-vs-future-self* distinction *also* activates these regions
- The magnitude of the mPFC/rACC differential between current-self and future-self predicts the individual's *temporal discount rate*
- Subjects with high future-self-continuity (less mPFC differential) discount future rewards less, save more

The translation: **the brain treats future-self as a partial-stranger by default; reducing the partial-stranger-treatment (raising future-self-continuity) flattens the discount curve and increases inter-temporal investment**.

Mathematically: if *β* is the future-self-continuity coefficient and *δ(t)* is the discount factor at time *t*:

$$\delta(t) = \delta_0 \cdot \exp(-\lambda t) + \beta \cdot (1 - \exp(-\lambda t))$$

Higher *β* asymptotes the discount factor to a value > 0; lower *β* lets it decay to 0. The Hershfield finding: making future-self vivid (via aged-photographs, future-self-letters, mPFC-engagement) raises *β*, raises the asymptote, motivates investment.
